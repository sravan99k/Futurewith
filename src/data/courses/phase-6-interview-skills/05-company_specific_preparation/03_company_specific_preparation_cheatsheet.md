## Behavioral Question Templates

### Amazon-Specific Questions

```
"TELL ME ABOUT A TIME WHEN YOU HAD TO DELIVER RESULTS UNDER PRESSURE"
Framework:
- Situation: High-pressure business context
- Task: Specific responsibility and timeline
- Action: Steps taken under pressure, decisions made
- Result: Delivered outcomes, lessons learned
- Amazon connection: Customer impact, ownership

"TELL ME ABOUT A TIME WHEN YOU HAD TO INVENT AND SIMPLIFY"
Framework:
- Situation: Complex process or system challenge
- Task: Need to simplify while maintaining effectiveness
- Action: Creative problem-solving, trade-offs considered
- Result: Simplified solution with better outcomes
- Amazon connection: Bias for action, high standards
```

### Google-Specific Questions

```
"TELL ME ABOUT A TIME WHEN YOU HAD TO LEARN SOMETHING NEW QUICKLY"
Framework:
- Situation: Learning requirement and timeline
- Task: Become proficient in new technology/skill
- Action: Learning strategy, resources used, practice
- Result: Proficiency achieved, application to work
- Google connection: Learning agility, technical excellence

"TELL ME ABOUT A TECHNICAL CHALLENGE YOU SOLVED THROUGH INNOVATION"
Framework:
- Situation: Technical problem with limited solutions
- Task: Find innovative approach or breakthrough
- Action: Research, experimentation, creative thinking
- Result: Novel solution, technical advancement
- Google connection: Innovation, global impact
```

### Meta-Specific Questions

```
"TELL ME ABOUT A TIME WHEN YOU HAD TO MOVE FAST ON A PROJECT"
Framework:
- Situation: Time-sensitive project with clear deadline
- Task: Deliver results quickly without sacrificing quality
- Action: Rapid iteration, prioritization, risk management
- Result: On-time delivery with quality maintained
- Meta connection: Move fast, focus on impact

"TELL ME ABOUT A TIME WHEN YOU HAD TO USE DATA TO INFLUENCE A DECISION"
Framework:
- Situation: Decision requiring data-driven approach
- Task: Gather, analyze, and present compelling data
- Action: Data collection, analysis, stakeholder presentation
- Result: Data-driven decision, positive outcome
- Meta connection: Data-driven, technical excellence
```

### Startup-Specific Questions

```
"TELL ME ABOUT A TIME WHEN YOU HAD TO FIGURE SOMETHING OUT WITH NO GUIDANCE"
Framework:
- Situation: New challenge with limited support/resources
- Task: Find solution independently and quickly
- Action: Research, experimentation, creative problem-solving
- Result: Successful solution, learning applied elsewhere
- Startup connection: Scrappy, learning agility

"TELL ME ABOUT A TIME WHEN YOU HAD TO DO WORK OUTSIDE YOUR NORMAL ROLE"
Framework:
- Situation: Team need that extended beyond your scope
- Task: Take on additional responsibilities or roles
- Action: Step up, learn new skills, deliver results
- Result: Team success, expanded capabilities
- Startup connection: Wearing multiple hats, team first
```

## Salary Negotiation Quick Guide

### Big Tech Compensation Structure

```
BASE SALARY:
Research Range: levels.fyi, Glassdoor, Blind
Negotiation Strategy: Focus on total compensation, not just base

EQUITY COMPENSATION:
Type: RSUs (most common at public companies)
Vesting: 4 years, 1-year cliff standard
Value: Calculate based on current stock price

BONUSES:
Annual: Typically 10-20% of base
Signing: Often offered to bridge equity gaps
Performance: Based on individual/company performance

NEGOTIATION TACTICS:
- "I'm excited about the role. Based on my research, I was hoping to discuss compensation around [X] for total compensation"
- "I have another offer at [higher amount]. Could you help me understand if there's flexibility on total compensation?"
- "I value the growth opportunities here. Can we discuss accelerating the equity vesting schedule?"
```

### Startup Compensation Considerations

```
EQUITY FOCUS:
Options vs RSUs: Understand tax implications
Percentage: 0.1% - 5%+ depending on stage and role
Acceleration: Single vs double trigger clauses

CASH COMPENSATION:
Below market: 10-30% discount common
Benefits: Creative perks and flexible arrangements
Career: Focus on growth and learning opportunities

NEGOTIATION STRATEGY:
- "I'm excited about the mission. Given the equity upside potential, I'd like to discuss base salary alignment with market rates"
- "What would it take to increase the equity package to [specific percentage]?"
- "Can we discuss performance-based bonus opportunities as the company grows?"
```

### Negotiation Timeline

```
WEEK 1: Initial Offer
- Thank them and request written offer
- Ask clarifying questions about compensation details
- Research and compare with market rates

WEEK 2: Counter-Proposal
- Present specific counter-offers with rationale
- Focus on total compensation and growth opportunities
- Maintain positive and collaborative tone

WEEK 3: Final Negotiation
- Finalize terms and get everything in writing
- Address any remaining concerns
- Express enthusiasm and commitment
```

## Interview Day Preparation

### 2 Hours Before Interview

```
□ Review company research and key talking points
□ Practice your elevator pitch (30 seconds)
□ Prepare 3-5 STAR stories for likely questions
□ Test technology setup (video, audio, internet)
□ Prepare questions to ask each interviewer
□ Review your resume and recent achievements
□ Set up professional environment (lighting, background)
□ Have water and notepad ready
□ Review salary expectations and negotiation points
□ Practice confident body language and posture
```

### During Interview

```
□ Listen to the complete question before responding
□ Take a moment to organize your thoughts
□ Use the appropriate framework (STAR/CAR/SOAR)
□ Be specific with examples and quantifiable results
□ Ask clarifying questions if needed
□ Show enthusiasm and genuine interest
□ Take notes on key points and next steps
□ Maintain eye contact and engaged body language
□ End with thoughtful questions about the role/team
□ Thank each interviewer and confirm next steps
```

### Questions to Ask Interviewers

```
FOR HIRING MANAGERS:
- "What are the biggest technical challenges facing the team?"
- "How do you measure success in this role?"
- "What does the first 90 days look like for someone in this position?"

FOR TEAM MEMBERS:
- "What do you enjoy most about working here?"
- "How would you describe the team culture and collaboration?"
- "What opportunities are there for growth and development?"

FOR EXECUTIVES:
- "Where do you see the company in the next 3-5 years?"
- "What are the key priorities for this role in driving company success?"
- "How do you balance innovation with stability and reliability?"
```

## Red Flags to Avoid

### Content Red Flags

```
❌ VAGUE EXAMPLES
Avoid: "I worked on a big project"
Better: "I led a team of 6 engineers to migrate our monolithic application to microservices, reducing deployment time from 2 hours to 15 minutes"

❌ NO COMPANY RESEARCH
Avoid: "Tell me about your company"
Better: "I'm excited about your recent launch of [product feature]. How is it performing relative to your expectations?"

❌ SALARY TALKING TOO EARLY
Avoid: Asking about compensation in first interview
Better: "I'd love to learn more about the role responsibilities first, then we can discuss compensation"

❌ BADMOUTHING PREVIOUS EMPLOYERS
Avoid: "My last company was terrible because..."
Better: "I learned a lot there, but I'm excited about new opportunities to..."
```

### Communication Red Flags

```
❌ INTERRUPTING THE INTERVIEWER
- Wait for complete questions before responding
- Take time to think before answering
- Ask for clarification if needed

❌ BEING TOO TECHNICAL FOR THE AUDIENCE
- Adapt technical depth to interviewer's background
- Use analogies and examples when appropriate
- Focus on business impact over technical details

❌ LACK OF AUTHENTICITY
- Don't memorize responses completely
- Show genuine interest and curiosity
- Be honest about your experience and growth areas

❌ NOT ASKING QUESTIONS
- Always prepare 3-5 questions per interviewer
- Show curiosity about role, team, and company
- Demonstrate you've done your research
```

### Cultural Red Flags

```
BIG TECH:
- Not showing scale or global impact thinking
- Lack of data-driven decision making examples
- No evidence of technical leadership or mentorship

STARTUP:
- Seeming too rigid or process-heavy
- Not showing flexibility or adaptability
- Lack of enthusiasm for mission or growth

GENERAL:
- Poor communication or collaboration examples
- No evidence of learning or growth mindset
- Negative attitude toward challenges or change
```

## Emergency Interview Fixes

### If You're Drawing a Blank

```
BUY TIME STRATEGIES:
"That's a great question. Let me think of the best example..."
"I'm excited about this opportunity. Let me share a relevant experience..."
"This reminds me of a situation where I..."

FRAMEWORK FALLBACKS:
- "I'll use the STAR method to structure my response..."
- "Let me break this down into the key components..."
- "There are a few aspects to address here..."
```

### If You Go Off Track

```
RECOVERY PHRASES:
"Let me focus on the most relevant aspect..."
"To directly answer your question about [topic]..."
"I want to make sure I address your core concern about..."

COURSE CORRECTION:
- Acknowledge you're getting off topic
- Refocus on the original question
- Provide a more targeted response
```

### If You Make a Mistake

```
MISTAKE HANDLING:
"That's not quite right. Let me correct that..."
"I misspoke. What I meant to say was..."
"Actually, let me reframe that example..."

RECOVERY STRATEGIES:
- Don't over-apologize or dwell on the mistake
- Provide the correct information
- Maintain confidence and continue
- Use it as a learning opportunity
```

### If Technical Questions Are Too Hard

```
HONESTY APPROACH:
"I'm not familiar with that specific technology, but I'd approach it by..."
"That would be outside my current expertise, but I have experience with similar..."
"I'm not the right person for that specific question, but I can speak to related areas..."

DEMONSTRATE PROBLEM-SOLVING:
- Explain your approach to learning new technologies
- Show how you've tackled unfamiliar challenges
- Emphasize adaptability and learning agility
- Connect to relevant experience you do have
```

## Common Confusions

### 1. Cheatsheet vs. Deep Preparation Balance

**Confusion**: Relying too heavily on cheatsheets vs. developing genuine understanding.
**Resolution**: Use cheatsheets for quick reference and last-minute review, but invest time in deep company research and authentic story development. Cheatsheets should supplement, not replace, thorough preparation.

### 2. One-Size-Fits-All Approach

**Confusion**: Using the same preparation strategy for all companies regardless of their unique characteristics.
**Resolution**: Customize your approach based on company type (big tech vs. startup), stage (early vs. late), and culture (technical vs. business focused). Use cheatsheets as starting points, then add company-specific depth.

### 3. Memorization vs. Understanding

**Confusion**: Memorizing cheatsheet content vs. understanding underlying principles.
**Resolution**: Focus on understanding WHY certain approaches work for specific companies rather than memorizing specific phrases. Adapt cheatsheet guidance to your authentic experience and communication style.

### 4. Information Overload

**Confusion**: Trying to absorb too much cheatsheet information simultaneously.
**Resolution**: Create a simplified personal cheatsheet with only the most critical information for each company. Use the full cheatsheet as reference material, not memorization material.

### 5. Generic vs. Authentic Examples

**Confusion**: Using cheatsheet templates without personalizing them to your actual experience.
**Resolution**: Map cheatsheet frameworks to your real experiences. If you don't have an example that fits a specific framework, either develop a related experience or acknowledge the area for future growth rather than fabricating examples.

### 6. Timing of Cheatsheet Usage

**Confusion**: When to reference cheatsheets during actual interviews.
**Resolution**: Use cheatsheets for pre-interview preparation and practice, but rely on natural conversation during interviews. The goal is to have the information internalized so you can access it naturally without appearing rehearsed.

### 7. Salary Negotiation Template Application

**Confusion**: Applying cheatsheet negotiation scripts directly without adaptation to your specific situation.
**Resolution**: Use cheatsheet negotiation strategies as frameworks, then customize based on your market research, competing offers, and personal priorities. Authenticity in negotiation conversations is crucial.

### 8. Cultural Fit Misinterpretation

**Confusion**: Attempting to mirror company values exactly as stated rather than finding genuine alignment.
**Resolution**: Use cheatsheets to identify potential value alignments, then focus on authentic examples from your experience that demonstrate those values naturally. Forced alignment is usually obvious to experienced interviewers.
